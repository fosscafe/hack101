Digital Transformation Using Node.js

This Swiss Army Knife Is More Than Just An Application Platform

November 10, 2016

By Michael Facemire with Martin Gill , Jaclyn Galan , Andrew Reese
Table of Contents

Why read this report

Node.js the tool of choice for digital upstarts in Silicon Valley â€” but application development and delivery (AD&D) pros in enterprises around the world are successfully using it to transform their businesses as well. This report details five common uses cases for Node and how it can underpin digital transformation.

Key Takeaways

Node.js Is Enterprise Ready

Node.js is no longer just a platform for super-cool digital startups. Organizations such as Capital One, Lowe's, and NASA have embraced it as an enterprise-scale platform.

Node.js Powers Digital Transformation

Digital transformations require large technology shifts. Node.js enables companies to reduce the risk of these transformations.

Node.js Is More Than Just An Application Platform

Node.js enables rapid experimentation with corporate data, application modernization, and even drives internet of things (IoT) solutions.

MAKE NODE.JS YOUR DIGITAL TRANSFORMATION APPLICATION PLATFORM
Consumers are adopting more things more quickly â€” a trend that Forrester has termed "hyperadoption," the rapid, simultaneous uptake of unprecedented behaviors. (see endnote 1) Like life-long gamblers, hoping for that one big score that changes their lives forever, businesses crave innovation in hopes of dropping into the hyperadoption current; they want to create the next Angry Birds or Pokemon Go, or become the "Ã¼ber for (insert industry here)." Bad gamblers go all in and place one big bet on one big game, hoping for the best. Companies that make successful digital transformations know that taking many smaller chances on new digital experiences and being ready to immediately go all in when one takes off is the better option.
"If you want to have good ideas you must have many ideas." (Linus Pauling, Nobel Peace Prize and Nobel Prize in Chemistry recipient)
But how can AD&D pros deliver this rapid innovation when software development takes so long and is rife with so many unknown roadblocks? The answer is they can't â€” not with the old way of building software. As Linus detailed about ideas, we must build new digital experiences quickly and be prepared to throw them away if our customers show no signs of adoption. This requires a new application development platform â€” Node.js is quickly becoming that platform. (see endnote 2)

Node.js Does More Than Just Host Applications
While Node is fast becoming the application platform of choice for building digital experiences that can react to hyperadoptive consumer behavior, its adoption is spreading far beyond bearded hipsters in Shoreditch lofts. AD&D pros in enterprise-scale businesses are realizing a number of additional benefits as they adopt it â€” and not just from a pure technology advantage. Forrester's Global Business TechnographicsÂ® Developer Survey, 2016 shows that developers who have built applications using Node are 18 percentage points more likely to want to become a guru in the development community over those that haven't used Node ( see Figure 1 ). Their passion for this technology invigorates enthusiasm far beyond their own development team. But wait, there are more use cases for Node in the enterprise, because Node.js is enabling developers to:
  • Build APIs that support both application and experience demands. "APIs enable innovation" â€” the statement business leaders hear daily in the technology press. Yet the realities are more nuanced: Front-end developers need APIs that support device and screen-size contexts, whereas back-end developers need APIs that enable high-performance data reads and writes. Backend-for-frontend (BFF) is an emerging architectural pattern used to develop applications that support multiple digital front-ends ( see Figure 2 ). Node.js is an outstanding platform for the front-end side of this equation because of the natural JavaScript language synergy; it excels in the back-end side because of its ability to perform at scale.
  • For example: Capital One uses Node.js in its aggregation tier, or what it refers to as its "orchestration layer." Historically, this was built with Java, but augmenting this with Node allows Capital One to share code across multiple web applications, regardless of the front-end technology. This enables developers to build web apps with both Angular and React and have these front-end developers do back-end data transformation work, given that both layers are written in JavaScript.
  • Rapidly experiment with new and existing corporate data. Hackathons are a great way to bring a fresh perspective to a business challenge: Get a group of developers together, give them a challenge, then sit back and watch the magic of ad hoc development happen. (see endnote 3) Want to watch that magic grind to a halt? Force those developers to ask the IT team for access to data, access credentials, and details on working with your proprietary data formats. A thin Node.js proxy layer on top of these disparate systems directly solves this problem. It gives developers direct access to data, at a single federated access point, on a platform that allows them to mash up that data to support their unique scenario.
  • For example: A major US retail bank realized the innovative opportunities offered by hosting an internal hackathon, but struggled with logistics as developers all had to work with "the guy" who knew how to access the data they needed. It addressed this by creating an API platform, built on Node.js, which allowed this in-demand developer to build connectors through the API layer, vastly boosting success of the follow-on hackathons.
  • Accelerate application modernization. Data hidden in legacy systems inhibits or even halts development of new digital interfaces. Many corporate business processes are built with mainframe applications that charge per processing cycle, or have created massive monoliths over time due to technology accretion. Mikeal Rogers of the Node.js Foundation states that, "by disconnecting the front- and back-end, it allows developers to integrate new data sources that weren't initially used, creating an opportunity to extend the reach of their product." Node enables developers to create new customer experiences without rebuilding applications.
  • For example: Lowe's has had an online eCommerce presence for many years, but years of additive construction to support new business and new device needs created an application monolith that required a complete rebuild (and associated downtime) to make simple changes. Rick Adams, manager of IT application portfolio, stated, "The risk behind making a simple text change on the home page or a product listing became such a daunting thing that our business and IT people weren't comfortable keeping up with demand." Lowe's inserted Node.js on top of the services layer that supported this legacy app and was quickly and easily able to completely redesign the two most highly trafficked pages on the site, which now both run on Node full time. (see endnote 4)
  • Create digital experiences on all platforms â€” not just mobile and web. Innovative customer experiences are often best served with a native desktop client. Apple sold more than 5.7 million new Macs in the Q4 2015 â€” people aren't paying a premium on these devices simply to get access to a web browser. (see endnote 5) But 84% of global developers in North American and Europe aren't coding in Swift and Objective-C, the native languages to MacOS, so how will these thick-client apps get built? (see endnote 6) Enter Electron, a framework built on Node.js for creating native Mac, Windows, and Linux applications using HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. Now the same developers that innovate on desktop and mobile web experiences can extend to build rich native client experiences as well.
  • For example: Messaging platforms are currently a hot space for innovation, and you'd be hard-pressed to find a development team that doesn't use Slack for communication. Slack's success is driven by being available wherever users are, on any variety of mobile or desktop device. Slack built its desktop client using Electron, enabling it to use the underlying operating system capabilities while leveraging web platform development skills. Microsoft's latest text editor, Visual Studio Code, followed the same model to rapid success.
  • Innovate on future connected device experiences. Node.js is not limited to request/response network transaction models. Many internet of things (IoT) devices rely on real-time streaming, using data protocols such as web sockets, MQTT, or AMQP. This data must be processed in real time, which is where Node excels. Need further evidence of developers choosing it for IoT? Socket.io, a Node module for building streaming applications, is the fourth-most-popular starred npm module as of August 2016, and developers have already created frameworks for building IoT on top of Node, like cylon.js. (see endnote 7)
  • For example: Skycatch, a company that helps manage drone data, builds drones running Node on Intel Edison chips to help construction companies manage job sites. The captured data is sent to a back-end, also running Node, for processing. Node.js works well in constrained environments because of its small footprint and execution environment requirements. Developers have adopted IoT platforms like the Raspberry Pi and Intel Edison at an alarming pace, largely because they can build applications using common web skills, running on Node.

Survey Methodology
Forrester's Global Business Technographics Developer Survey, 2016 was fielded in January 2016. This online survey included 1,867 respondents in Australia, Brazil, Canada, China, France, Germany, India, New Zealand, the UK, and the US.
Forrester's Business Technographics ensures that the final survey population contains only those with significant involvement in the planning, funding, and purchasing of business and technology products and services. ResearchNow fielded this survey on behalf of Forrester. Survey respondent incentives include points redeemable for gift certificates.
Please note that the brand questions included in this survey should not be used to measure market share. The purpose of Forrester's Business Technographics brand questions is to show usage of a brand by a specific target audience at one point in time.
